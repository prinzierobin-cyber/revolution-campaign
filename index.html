Campaign Manifesto — 2027

The most revolutionary thing a person can do today is live authentically.

After ODYSSEY, we discovered who we are. After SUPER NATURE, we rediscovered our connection to ourselves, each other, and the natural world. REVOLUTION is the moment everything clicks into place.

It is the moment we stop asking for permission.

  • Not permission to dress differently
  • Not permission to create differently
  • Not permission to love differently
  • Not permission to live differently

Because once you know who you are, and once you understand where you belong, there is only one question left: What is stopping you from being that person?

For many people, the answer is permission. Permission from society. From family. From culture. From strangers.

REVOLUTION is about rejecting that need for approval and embracing the freedom of self-expression.

The Hero Visual

The Trans­formation

She is not becoming someone new. She is finally becoming who she was always meant to be.

The hero visual captures the exact moment she steps into the most authentic version of herself — a person moving through an invisible dimension, a symbolic space between who she was and who she is becoming.

It feels as though she is shifting into a higher frequency. Not through transformation into someone new, but through the release of limitations, expectations, and the need for external validation.

The glowing worlds surrounding her represent portals into a more authentic state of being — where instinct replaces approval and self-expression replaces conformity.

As she moves through these dimensions, she becomes one with her spirit animal. Not as a literal creature, but as a symbol of her most natural, confident, and uninhibited self.

ODYSSEY was about discovering yourself.
SUPER NATURE was about reconnecting with your true nature.
REVOLUTION is about embodying it.

They are no longer becoming. They are arriving.

The Philosophy

From Punk to Animal Print

The Visual Heritage

The visual language of REVOLUTION began with research into early punk and emo culture. One recurring visual code was the striped sleeve — a recognizable symbol of rebellion, self-expression, and belonging to alternative communities.

For REVOLUTION, that symbol has been reinterpreted. The striped sleeve evolves into oversized animal prints. The spirit remains the same: a visual statement made by people who choose their own path.

The animal prints become extensions of identity. A way of channeling confidence, individuality, and instinct through fashion.

Energy, Not Perfection

Why It Feels Different

The bright neon palette draws inspiration from electronic music culture, festivals, nightlife, internet aesthetics, and the highly visual world Gen Z grew up in. The colours feel energetic, optimistic, playful, and impossible to ignore.

Many lifestyle brands today take themselves too seriously. Everything feels curated. Everything feels controlled. Everything feels perfect. But Gen Z doesn't connect with perfection.

They connect with personality. They connect with authenticity. They connect with brands that are willing to experiment, participate, and have fun. REVOLUTION embraces bold colours, animal prints, charms, stickers, and customization because self-expression should never feel intimidating. It should feel alive.

The Collection

The four visual worlds become the foundation for four distinct eyewear families. Rather than designing one collection with different colours, each world creates its own design language.

Catalogue Design

Affordable Luxury,
Printed in Neon

The cover is the first statement. A bright neon-pastel tartan printed with neon pastel inks — bold enough to stop you, considered enough to hold you. It doesn't whisper. It announces.

Inside, the logic shifts. Each spread follows a strict Swiss grid: one page of clean, typeset content; one page given entirely to a single image. White space does the heavy lifting. The neon pastel ink bleeds through as an accent, not a takeover.

The restraint is intentional. A catalogue that goes full Y2K loses the sense of luxury. This one earns it — by knowing exactly when to be quiet. The result feels like something you'd find in a high-end boutique that also has a sense of humour.

Logo & Typography

The Logo

Chrome. Metallic. Y2K.

The custom KOMONO logo was created specifically for this campaign. Inspired by early 2000s punk, skate, emo, and alternative culture — but reinterpreted through a more optimistic and contemporary lens. Chrome and metallic effects, neon pink highlights, aggressive stretched letterforms. Imperfect by design rather than overly polished.

The Typeface

Custom. Aggressive. Self-made.

A custom typeface was developed for the campaign — sharing the same DNA as the logo. Sharp, angular letterforms with an imperfect quality that echoes the campaign's message: authenticity over perfection. Built to be used across apparel graphics, campaign materials, and the digital playground.

Design Philosophy

Rebellious. Energetic. Yours.

The elongated K flowing into the O creates tension and movement. The logo should feel rebellious, energetic, and self-made — as though someone made it themselves rather than commissioned it from an agency. That's intentional. The campaign celebrates self-expression, and the identity starts there.

Product as Canvas

For Gen Z, No Permission Needed isn't a rallying cry — it's already their baseline. Their open-mindedness, their fluid identity, their refusal to justify themselves: none of that feels radical to them. It's just how they move through the world. That's exactly what makes the title land. It doesn't preach to them. It recognises them. The revolution isn't something they need to join. They've been living it.

Two product categories. Four prints. One shared language. The collection doesn't decorate the campaign — it is the campaign, made wearable.

Animal print is not a trend.
It is a visual symbol of authenticity.

"They represent people who no longer hide, no longer blend in and no longer ask for permission to be themselves."

Animal prints were chosen not because they are rebellious, but because they symbolise instinctive self-expression. Throughout history they have been associated with confidence, visibility and individuality.

Within REVOLUTION they become a continuation of SUPER NATURE — carrying the power of nature into human expression. The trilogy closes here. ODYSSEY found identity. SUPER NATURE found connection to the natural world. REVOLUTION wears it.

Each print is a different kind of visibility. The zebra refuses to blend. The tiger moves with total conviction. The cheetah is the fastest version of itself. The tartan is an awakening — to yourself, and to love. Together they make a collection that doesn't ask whether the wearer fits in — it assumes they never wanted to.

The Patch System

A vision board you wear. Built patch by patch.

The collection includes 25 patches — each with its own print, colour, and visual identity. Their colours and aesthetic energy are rooted in the campaign's manifesto and four visual worlds, but each patch exists in its own playful register.

What makes the system work is that each patch carries a hidden meaning. Not printed on the patch itself — discovered on the sticker sheet inside the Revolution Pack, or revealed on the website at launch. You choose a patch because something draws you to it visually. You find out what it means after.

That discovery is the moment. The combination you build on your shirt becomes your own vision board — a set of intentions you've chosen to carry and wear. Some people pick one and mean it completely. Some layer five. No combination is wrong. No two shirts end up the same.

Campaign manifesto — source of each intention
Sticker sheet — where the meanings are revealed
Y2K landing page — full patch library at drop
Apparel as canvas — shirt is complete only when you decide it is
Paper mockups in the pack — rehearse your build at home
Charms on eyewear — same system, different surface

25 patches. 25 intentions. Infinite combinations. The shirt is the canvas. The patches are the language. What you choose to say is entirely up to you.

Every KOMONO campaign launch has been defined as much by where it happened as by what happened. The location is not a backdrop — it becomes part of the campaign's identity. People remember the place. They reference it. The venue choice is a creative decision, not a logistical one.

REVOLUTION's outdoor setting continues that tradition while making the campaign's most important argument: this is not an exclusive event behind a guest list. The sky is the ceiling. The community is the guest list.

Why Outdoor — The Strategic Decision

Gen Z Doesn't Go to Clubs.

That's not a criticism. It's a cultural fact that most brands are still getting wrong in 2027. The millennial generation built nightlife culture — the dark room, the velvet rope, the DJ booth as status symbol. Gen Z rejected it. They go to markets, parks, outdoor festivals, rooftop gatherings, beach hang-outs. They want to see each other's faces. They want natural light in their photos. They want to leave when they want to leave. They want the air to feel free.

A club venue for this campaign would contradict every word of the manifesto. No permission needed — and yet you need to be on the list, you need to look a certain way, you need to survive the queue. An outdoor location removes all of that. It is the venue choice as campaign statement.

Authentic experiences photograph differently too. Golden hour light on the beach creates content no studio can replicate. The community portraits from this event will circulate for months because they look like real life, not a production.

The Venue — What We Need

Open Air. Covered Zone. Community Feel.

Beach or open-air outdoor space as the main stage. The majority of the event lives outside — the DJ, the Grandma Station, the portrait studio, the photobooth. The covered zone handles what outdoor can't: the Customisation Lab, the production area, weather contingency, the quieter community spaces.

The covered area should not feel like a tent at a corporate event. It should feel like someone threw a tarpaulin over a creative space and filled it with people. The same DIY energy as the campaign itself.

Why the Grandma Works — and Why She Becomes an Icon

She is not a prop. She is not irony. She is the campaign's most honest proof of concept.

The entire campaign asks one question: who gave you permission? She answers it without saying a word. Pink hair. Her own style. Her own terms. Nobody handed her permission to look the way she looks, to show up the way she shows up, to press shirts at a youth campaign at her age — she simply never asked for it. That is the campaign. That is the whole campaign, standing in front of you.

She works because she is the most unexpected version of the campaign's audience. Everyone who walks through the door is there to express themselves — and then they see her, and they realise the campaign was never about youth. It was never about a generation. It was about a decision. A decision anyone can make at any age. The moment people understand that, the campaign becomes bigger than a product launch.

She appears twice. First in-store during the March launch weekend, pressing shirts and meeting people picking up their packs. Then at the Revolution Party in May, as the emotional centrepiece of the night. The repetition builds her into a character. By the party, people who met her in March will point her out. People who didn't will ask who she is. Both conversations are exactly what the campaign needs.

She will be photographed thousands of times. She will be posted, shared, and remembered long after the event ends. Not because she was designed to go viral — but because she is genuinely, unmistakably herself. That is why she is an icon.

Did this campaign grow the audience, or just entertain the existing one? If the number is flat, the campaign worked for fans. If it grew, it worked as a brand-building exercise.

Content Release Calendar

Each phase has a content job. The timing isn't arbitrary — it's engineered so that each wave of content creates the appetite for the next. The street interviews build the question. The question makes the pack feel like an answer. The pack generates the submissions. The submissions fuel the event. The event creates the afterglow. Nothing drops randomly.

January

Phase 01 — The Question

Plant the question before anyone knows what it belongs to. 3 weeks of intrigue, 1 week of identity reveal. Never explain — let people wonder.

Wk 1–2 / TikTok + Reels
Teaser visuals — no context
3× per week. No caption, no brand tag. Just the image or short clip. Let the mystery do the work.
Tue / Thu / Sat — 19:00–21:00
Wk 3 / TikTok
First street interview drops
2× per week. Lead with the most surprising answer. The question appears on screen. No brand logo until episode 3.
Wed + Fri — 18:00–20:00
Wk 3–4 / Instagram Feed
Campaign identity reveal
2× per week. The REVOLUTION wordmark + NO PERMISSION NEEDED. Still no product. Brand comes before product.
Tue + Thu — 09:00–11:00
Wk 4 / Paid
Boost top interview
Take the strongest performing interview and boost it. Target: lookalike audience of existing followers + interest-based Gen Z / youth culture.
Budget: €800–1,200 / 7 days

February

Phase 02 — The Invitation

PR drop week is the single most important content moment of the first half. Every piece of content this month exists to make the pack feel unmissable.

Wk 1 / All channels
Pack reveal — owned content
Show the pack. Every element. Do it in a 15-second flat-lay video. No music. Just the objects. Let the craft speak.
Tuesday — 12:00 drop
Wk 2 / Creator content
First creator unboxings go live
Don't post your own content this week. Step back. Let creator content lead. Repost and amplify — don't compete with it.
Organic — let creators choose timing
Wk 3–4 / TikTok + Reels
Interviews continue + first designs appear
2× interviews per week. Feature the first community designs alongside them. Show the campaign working. 3–4 posts per week total.
Wed / Fri — 18:00–20:00
Wk 2 / Paid
Amplify best creator content
Take 2 creator posts (with permission) and run as paid dark posts. Creator-authentic content outperforms brand content in paid by 2–3×.
Budget: €1,500–2,000 / 2 weeks

March

Phase 03 — The Playground

Retail goes live. Digital playground launches. Content job this month: make participation feel normal. Show people doing it, not just the invitation to do it.

Launch weekend / Stories
Grandma Station in-store — live
Stories only. Raw, unedited. Show the grandma pressing shirts. Show real people's faces when they meet her. Post 6–8 stories across the day.
Saturday — all day from 11:00
Wk 2–4 / TikTok + Reels
UGC reposts + playground demo
Start reposting community designs. Show the Y2K playground in use. 4× per week. The brand becomes a curator, not a broadcaster.
Mon / Wed / Fri / Sun — 18:00–21:00
Wk 1 / Digital
Y2K playground launch
Announce on all channels simultaneously. One reel showing the tool in action. Link in bio. Stories with swipe-up. Email to existing community.
Wednesday — 10:00 drop
Wk 1–4 / Paid
Retarget + acquisition push
Retarget people who visited the site but didn't design. Acquisition campaign targeting youth culture / DIY / streetwear audiences. Drive to playground.
Budget: €2,500–3,500 / month

April

Phase 04 — The Selection

Announce the event. Build personal anticipation. This month's content should make people feel like they know the community already — so attending feels like a reunion, not a cold room.

Wk 1 / All channels
THE MOMENT series launches
3× per week. 15 seconds each. Back-to-back in the same week for impact — then slow to 2× per week. These run alongside ticket sales the whole month.
Tue / Thu / Sat — 19:00–21:00
Wk 2 / All channels
Event announcement
One post. Maximum impact. Date, location teaser (outdoor — exact venue TBC). Ticket link. Reels format. Then one story per day counting down.
Wednesday — 12:00 drop
Wk 2–4 / Feed + Stories
Featured design reveals
2× per week. Each post introduces a designer. Name, face, their moment. The community meets the people behind the shirts they built.
Mon + Thu — 09:00–11:00
Wk 2–4 / Paid
Event ticket campaign
Drive ticket sales. Use THE MOMENT content as the ad creative — it outperforms hard-sell event ads. Retarget website visitors + engagement audiences.
Budget: €2,000–3,000 / 3 weeks

May

Phase 05 — The Celebration

Event week is the campaign's peak. Content goes live in real time. Have 3 people posting throughout the day — one on stories, one on TikTok, one capturing for recap. Post within 6 hours of event ending.

Wk 1–2 / Build-up
Countdown + behind the scenes
Show setup. The venue. The grandma arriving. First shirts rolling off the press. Raw, unpolished. 1 post per day in the 7 days before the event.
Daily — 18:00–20:00
Event day / Stories
Live all-day coverage
Stories every 30–45 minutes from doors open to close. The grandma pressing shirts. Golden hour portraits. The DJ stage at sunset. Shirt collection moments.
From 15:00 — real time
Event day / TikTok + Reels
2–3 polished short clips live same day
The grandma in action. Best outfit at the event. Most unexpected moment. Edit on location. Post before midnight the same night — not the day after.
First post by 22:00 event night
Post-event / Paid
Boost best event content
Take the highest-performing organic post from the event and boost immediately. Strike while the algorithm is warm. Don't wait for the recap reel.
Budget: €1,500–2,000 / 5 days

June

Phase 06 — The Afterglow

Don't let the campaign die with the event. June's content job is to make people who weren't there wish they had been — and make people who were feel celebrated.

Wk 1 / All channels
Full recap reel
60–90 second edit. The whole night. Start with the grandma. End with shirts being worn. This is the campaign's most shareable piece of long-form content.
Post within 72h of event
Wk 1–2 / Feed
Community portrait gallery
One portrait per day for 10 days. Tag the person. Let them reshare. No caption needed beyond their name and "No Permission Needed."
Daily — 09:00
Wk 3 / All channels
Custom designs — open drop
Announce the limited drop. Show the shirts in the wild — on the people who designed them. Link to shop. One week of content around the release.
Monday — 10:00 drop
Wk 1 / Paid
Recap reel amplification
Boost the recap reel to people who engaged with campaign content but didn't attend. FOMO drives product interest. Then retarget with the open drop — anyone can still order.
Budget: €1,000–1,500 / 2 weeks
TikTok
When + How
  • Best daysTue, Thu, Sat — avoid Sunday drops for campaign content
  • Best window18:00–21:00 local time — evening scroll peak
  • Frequency3–5× per week during active phases, 1–2× maintenance
  • Format priorityUnder 30 seconds. Hook in the first 2 seconds or it's lost.
  • CaptionsOne line max. The question, not the answer.
Instagram Reels + Feed
When + How
  • Best daysMon, Wed, Fri for feed. Reels any day 18:00–21:00.
  • Best window09:00–11:00 for feed posts — morning browse
  • FrequencyFeed: 3–4× per week. Stories: daily during active phases
  • Stories cadence7–9am teaser + 7–9pm follow-up. Never just once a day.
  • Reels vs feedReels for reach. Feed for community. Don't conflate them.
Paid Social
Rules of Amplification
  • Never boost a post under 4 hours oldLet organic signals build first — the algorithm rewards it
  • Best ad creativeCreator content > brand content. Always. Ask for usage rights upfront.
  • Audience priorityRetargeting > lookalike > interest. In that order of efficiency.
  • Don't boost everything3–4 key moments across the campaign. Concentrate the spend.
  • Event windowPause all non-event ads 48h before the event. Focus the algorithm.
